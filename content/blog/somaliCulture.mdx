## Dhaanto

It originates from the Dowlad Deegaanka Somalida. This dance is the most popular Somali dance that is played nearly in every Somali festive celebration. The dhaanto was inspired by the camel and mimics the actions of the camel. The dance song was revived during the Dervish period in the early 20th century when it was used to raise the 'spirits' of soldiers and was often sung on horseback. It has strong connections to the Ogaden clan. (Somali museum)

## Saylici 

Originated from the town of Zeila in northern Somalia. It began as an Islamic chant and slowly developed into a dance. The lyrics still used remain as they were for hundreds of years, which are mostly Islamic-oriented. It has a strong connection to Samaroon (Gidabursi) clan more than anyone.

## Shirib
The word "Shirib" in particular refers to a gathering or a meeting where discussions or cultural presentations take place; therefore, it is commonly known as a place where social interactions or cultural events occur. In this context, it is also called a gathering or assembly for discussing or presenting cultural matters. Consequently, it is often referred to as a venue where social harmony is fostered or where cultural events and discussions are facilitated, and it may be interchangeably called "Shirib" or "Shirbayn." Shirib is a lively and rhythmic traditional Somali dance common in the South and Central Somalia especially Middle Shabelle, Hiiran, and Galgaduud.

## Wilwilo
One of the folk dances performed by the Somali pastoralists in the northwestern part of Somalia and Djibouti. It is characterised by the dancers who dance with spears and shields while the team-leader wears a dagger. This shows that in the past this dance was a war dance. Now it is performed on special occasions.

## Soogur

## Dawladamiin
This is a famous entertainment dance performed by the youth in leisure time. It is governed by a strict body of rules that the performing teams should adhere to. The name of the dance is an abbreviated from of «Dawley, a simpleton should not be treated like a gentleman».

## Istun
This a dance-battle in which two groups stand face to face, to fight with small sticks, which would not inflict any serious injuries or heavy casualties. It is part of the celebrations marking the arrival of a new year. In the very early days, the event was heavily surrounded by significant superstitions and myths, such as the belief that failure to stage the tournament would ontail misfortune and evil omens — the failure of crops followed by famine and the failure of women to give birth to boys.

## Awbatiikhow
‘The name of this folk dance is derived from the «Melon» fruit. ‘The Somalis enjoy eating melons. They like to look at its attractive colours, and to smell its aroma. In aadition they believe it is good for the stomach. Awbatiikhow is a romantic love dance performed in praise of girls. The soloist celebrates in poetry: the beauty of his beloved and attributes all the good qualities of the melon fruit to her.

## Shooba
‘The oldest of all Somali folk dances. It’s origin is believed to be in the ancient kingdom of Egypt. The form of this dance and the dressing of the performer, confirm the close historical and cultural ties between the Somalis and the pharaohs.
